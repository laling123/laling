NATIONAL SPORTS: ARNIS

What is Arnis?

Arnis, also known as Eskrima or Kali, is the national martial art and sport of the Philippines. It is a weapon-based fighting system that primarily focuses on stick fighting but also includes techniques for bladed weapons, empty-hand combat, joint locks, grappling, and self-defense. Arnis has been practiced for centuries and was originally used by early Filipino warriors for self-defense and tribal warfare. The main goal of Arnis is to develop self-discipline, physical fitness, and self-defense skills while preserving Filipino martial traditions. Beyond combat, Arnis plays a crucial role in preserving Filipino history, culture, and identity. It teaches discipline, focus, and adaptability, which are essential in both martial arts and everyday life. Practicing Arnis enhances physical fitness, agility, and reflexes, making it beneficial for overall health. It is also an effective means of self-defense, equipping individuals with practical skills to protect themselves. As part of the Philippine education curriculum, Arnis helps instill patriotism and appreciation for the countryâ€™s heritage among students. Internationally, it has influenced various martial arts, military training, and action choreography in films. More than just a sport, Arnis symbolizes the resilience, strength, and ingenuity of the Filipino people.

History of Arnis

Pre-Colonial Origins and Early Influences:


The roots of Arnis can be traced back to pre-Hispanic Filipino fighting techniques used in conflicts between various tribes and kingdoms. These techniques involved the use of sticks, knives, and other improvised weapons. The current form of Arnis, however, has been influenced by Spanish fencing techniques introduced during the colonial era, along with other Asian martial arts like Silat, brought by traders and settlers from the Malay Archipelago. Some scholars even theorize that Arnis may have ancient Indian roots, possibly influenced by the stick-based martial art Silambam

Spanish Colonial Era and the Weapon Ban:

The Spanish colonization of the Philippines significantly impacted Arnis. While some historians suggest a strong Spanish influence on the art, others argue that the Spanish names for techniques simply reflect the prevalent language of the time. What is certain is that the Spanish introduced their own weapon arts and recruited Filipino soldiers and mercenaries, who likely integrated their newly acquired skills into existing Filipino martial traditions. A decree banning the common people from carrying weapons like the Kampilan and Kris forced Arnis practitioners to adapt, using rattan sticks as substitutes and disguising the art as folk dances to preserve.

TIMELINE OF ARNIS


  • Pre-Colonial Era (Before 1521)- Arnis originated in the Visayan and Luzon islands of the Philippines, evolving from indigenous combat techniques used by tribes and kingdoms.

  • Spanish Colonial Era (1521-1898) 1521: Datu Lapu-Lapu, leader of Mactan, used Arnis to defeat Ferdinand Magellan in the Battle of Mactan. 1596: Spanish authorities discouraged the practice of Arnis, leading to its decline in popularity. 1764: Arnis was banned by Spanish overlords, forcing Filipinos to practice it secretly. 1853: The name "Arnis" replaced "Kali," which means "long knife" or "sword."

  • American Era (1898-1946)- 1898: The Philippines gained independence from Spain, leading to a resurgence of Arnis. Arnis masters began to systemize and codify Arnis techniques.

  • Post-World War II (1946-Present)- 1969: Remy Presas introduced "Modern Arnis" into the Philippine education system.

  • 1980s: Arnis tournaments were established to promote the sport.

  • 2009: Arnis was declared the national sport and martial art of the Philippines by the Philippine government.

  • 2010: Arnis became a physical education course and was included in the Palarong Pambansa (National Games)
